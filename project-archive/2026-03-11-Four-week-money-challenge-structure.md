That's how behavior change actually works, not the "here's a rigid plan, execute it perfectly" approach most challenges take.

I really like:
- "Light pass" in the Before section
- "Keep numbers approximate if needed" 
- The "If you get stuck" footer (very you)
- Specifying which tool to use when
- The optional spreadsheet callout

**Where I'd push it:**

1. **Make the anti-perfectionism even louder.** Right now it's whispered in phrases like "optionally" and "if needed." I'd make it structural. Something like:

> **Important:** You don't need to complete every page to get value from this challenge. If a prompt doesn't land, skip it. If spreadsheets make you want to throw your laptop, ignore them. The challenge works because you're paying attention, not because you filled in every blank.

2. **Address the "what if I spend?" elephant immediately.** This challenge isn't actually about not spending (even though that's the name). It's about noticing. So maybe add to the Before section:

> * Quick reminder: Spending during a no-spend challenge doesn't mean you failed. It means you get data about what happened and why. The point is awareness, not perfection.

3. **The Week 2-3 combo might be heavy.** You've got two weeks of daily pages plus wrap-ups plus weekly check-ins. That's when people quit. I'd either:
   - Split them into separate steps, OR

> **If you get stuck:**

> **How to use this roadmap:**
> Linear people: Follow steps 1→2→3→4.

6. **Shift some "complete/finish" language to "explore/try."** Not everywhere, but in a few key spots. For example:
   - "Complete 'Before You Begin'" → "Try the 'Before You Begin' exercise"

**The book version** is for people who:
- Print their workbooks and don't want to keep toggling to a browser
- Want to scribble notes in margins

- Want something you can update without re-issuing files
- Need the "stuck/overwhelmed" intervention moment
- Prefer clicking around to flipping pages

They serve **different moments in the user experience**, so redundancy isn't a bug—it's a feature.

## How to prevent version drift

This is the main risk of having both. Here's how to manage it:

1. **The site is the source of truth.**  
   When you update the challenge, update the site first.

2. **The book version is intentionally minimal.**  
   It's just "here's the order of operations." It doesn't have context, so there's less to keep in sync.

3. **Add a version note in the books:**  
   > This roadmap was last updated [Month Year]. For the most current version, visit [Canva site link].

4. **Build a simple update checklist:**  
   ```
   When I change the challenge structure:
   □ Update Canva site
   □ Update Workbook roadmap page (if structure changed)
   □ Update Journal roadmap page (if structure changed)
   ```

   If you only changed *context* (like rewording a section), you don't need to touch the books. Only update them if the **order or structure** changes.

> **Not sure where to start?** Use this interactive roadmap: [link to Canva site]

**In the books themselves (first page of roadmap):**

## One potential refinement

If you want to get *really* clever, you could make the **book version** focus on structure and the **site version** focus on emotional/motivational support.

So:

**Book roadmap = logistics**  
"Here's what to do in what order."

**Site roadmap = logistics + psychology**  
"Here's what to do in what order AND here's what to do when it feels terrible."

That way they're complementary rather than redundant. The book gets you oriented. The site gets you unstuck.

---

## Bottom line

Yes, put it in both. Make the book version **short and structural**. Make the site version **long and supportive**. Link between them. Update the site freely; only touch the books if the structure changes.

This gives people options without creating confusion, and it lets you iterate the experience without constantly re-issuing PDFs.
