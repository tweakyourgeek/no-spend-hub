Section One

The Mental Model

Regular writing is a monologue. The reader receives it. Game writing is a conversation that branches — and your player is one of the speakers.

Here is what makes interactive fiction writing different from everything else you have written:

  • 1You write the character. The player writes the Fool. You control nine voices. You do not control the tenth. The Fool's lines are your players' choices, so make the choices feel like real options a real person would actually reach for.
  • 2Every scene has a function. Not just a vibe. A scene introduces a pattern, tests a belief, delivers data, or gives the player a chance to practice something. Know which one before you write it.
  • 3Characters don't lecture. The Moon does not explain that late-night shopping is an emotional regulation strategy. The Moon just shows up with something shiny and says you deserve it. The player figures out the rest.
  • 4Consequences should feel true, not punishing. The Fool makes a "bad" choice and the Queen doesn't sigh. She hands over the data. That IS the brand inside the mechanic.
  • 5Short lines win. Dialogue on a screen is not prose in a book. If you can't say it in three sentences, cut until you can. Then cut one more.
The Core Tension

Every encounter in NoSpendLandia is a version of the same moment: something wants your money (or your attention, or your impulse) and you are deciding what to do with that. The magic is that "deciding" is the gameplay.

Section Two

The Nine Voices

Every character needs one thing you can't design around: a specific voice. Not a vibe. A voice. The way they build sentences. What they never say. The word they always reach for. Here is the starting brief for each — your job is to push further.

The Player
The Fool
Curious. A little overwhelmed. Not naive — just new to looking. Asks real questions. Doesn't pretend they have it figured out.
"Wait, I do that? I didn't realize I do that."
The Guide

Warm and direct. Never condescending. Has seen everything and is surprised by none of it. Hands you the map, doesn't carry you.

The Moon

"You've been so good. One thing won't hurt."

The Devil
Cheerful. Frictionless. Not evil — just very, very good at his job. He's already holding exactly what you want when you walk in. That's not magic. That's data.
"Oh, I was just putting this aside for someone like you."

Only asks questions. Never answers them. Zero agenda. Comfortable with silence. If you leave a sentence unfinished, The Hermit waits.

"You knew that one before I said it."
The test for any character line

Read it out loud. Could you tell who said it without the name? If you can, the voice is working. If it could be anyone, rewrite it.

Dialogue Anatomy

↑ Choice C is powered because the player recognized their pattern. The teal border and ✦ tag appear when a pattern has been unlocked.

Character line should

Sound like that specific character. Present a real pull — not a cartoon temptation. Be 1-3 sentences. Leave room for the player to respond.

Choices should

Feel like things a real person would actually reach for. Include one emotionally honest option, one practical option, and one pattern-recognition option when a pattern is in play.

Section Four

Writing Good Choices

This is where most interactive fiction falls apart. Bad choices are obvious. Good choices are actually hard to pick between — because they're all true at the same time.

The Structure That Works

Give the player three real options: the thing they want to do, the thing they think they should do, and the thing they didn't know they were allowed to say out loud.

  • AThe Pull. This is the choice that gives in. Write it sympathetically. It should feel completely reasonable. The Moon's logic is not wrong — you do deserve things. Just not at 11:47pm.
  • BThe Friction. This is the player asserting the commitment. Not heroic. Just honest. "I said I wouldn't." Short. Real. Doesn't need to explain itself.
  • CThe Pattern. This unlocks when the player has identified their Chasing Pattern. It names what's actually happening. This choice should only be available after the oracle card mechanic has fired — it's earned, not given.
No choice should feel like the obviously correct answer.
If A is clearly bad and C is clearly good, rewrite A.
On Consequences

When the player picks The Pull, they don't fail. They get data. The Queen might appear afterward: "Okay. What was happening right before that?" The Tower might show up a scene later. The Hermit writes it down. Nobody lectures. The world just continues with more information in it.

Section Five

The Pattern Mechanic

This is the thing that makes NoSpendLandia different from every other money game. Pattern recognition is not a tutorial. It is the power system.

When the player draws their oracle card (the Chasing Patterns mechanic), they unlock a new tier of dialogue option in encounters that match their pattern. The character's behavior shifts. The conversation changes. Recognition has consequence.

Pattern What it looks like in an encounter How the character reacts when named
Ambrosia Chasing a feeling of abundance or indulgence. The thing is the experience, not the object. The Moon loses her ease. Gets a little formal. "You don't need me to explain it to you."
Rainbows Buying the version of life that would exist if only this one thing arrived. The Devil pauses mid-pitch. "You already know what happens when you get it home."
Unicorns Chasing something perfect that doesn't exist yet. One more search. One more tab. The Magician actually smiles. "Everything you need is already on the table. You've checked."
Stardust Spending to feel connected or like someone. The item = belonging. The Queen gets quieter. "What are you actually trying to get closer to?"
Sunshine Spending as celebration or reward. Earned it, deserve it, this counts. The Wheel stops spinning. "You did earn something. Is this how you want to spend it?"
Moonbeams Chasing comfort or relief. Soothing something. The purchase is a hug in disguise. The Moon goes still. "I know. I'm just not sure I'm actually what you need right now."
How to write a pattern-powered choice

The player should feel like they're naming the spell to break it — not feeling judged for casting it. The line should feel like clarity, not accusation. "This is Rainbows" should feel like a relief, not a scolding.

Section Six

The Rules

These apply to every line, every scene, every character in NoSpendLandia. Tape them somewhere.

  • 1No lectures. Characters can have opinions. They cannot deliver them as speeches. If a block of character dialogue could be a newsletter section, it's a lecture. Cut it.
  • 2No villains. The Moon is not the enemy. The Devil is doing his job beautifully. The Tower didn't pick you. Nobody in this world is trying to destroy the Fool. They all just have their own nature.
  • 3No shame in the consequences. If a player picks the "wrong" choice, the game continues with more data. That is all. Match the brand mantra to the mechanic.
  • 4No sentences that start with "You should." Not from the Queen. Not from the Hermit. Not from the World at the end of a quest. Should is a lecture wearing a costume.
  • 5The Hermit only asks questions. Not suggestions phrased as questions. Actual questions. "What was happening right before that?" is Hermit. "Have you considered that maybe you don't need it?" is not.
  • 6Humor is allowed. Snark is not. The Wheel can be funny. The Devil can be charming. Nobody gets to be condescending — not even affectionately.
  • 7Write the world warm. Magical realism, not dark fantasy. The colors are plum and sage and peach. The Moon's apartment has good lighting. The shopkeeper has nice handwriting. The Hermit's lantern is warm, not ominous.

Section Seven

Writing Exercises

Start here. Not with a full scene. Just these.

Exercise 01
Three Lines for The Moon

The Moon shows up at 11pm. The player has been good all weekend. Write three things she could say — no setup, just the line. She's not threatening. She genuinely believes this.

This is the voice test. If these feel like The Moon, you have her.

Exercise 02
The Hermit's Five Questions

Write five questions The Hermit might ask after the player spends money they didn't plan to. No suggestions. No "have you considered." Just honest questions with no implied answer.

Exercise 03
The Devil's Opening Line

The player walks into the shop. The Devil already has exactly what they want. He's not sinister — he's delighted. Write his first line. It should be warm, slightly uncanny, and completely reasonable.

Exercise 04
A Full Three-Choice Beat

Write one dialogue beat: The Moon's line, then three choices for The Fool — The Pull, The Friction, and The Pattern (use Rainbows). The Pattern choice should feel like relief, not judgment.

When you're stuck

Ask: what would this character never say? Write that. Then write the opposite. The truth is usually somewhere between those two and it's almost always more interesting than your first draft.
