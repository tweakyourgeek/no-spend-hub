Scene Overview

What This Scene Does

This is the first scene in the No-Spend Weekend quest. The Fool arrives at the Moon's apartment on Friday night — the exact moment the real game begins. The scene does three things:

Function
Introduces the pull of a Friday night impulse buy
Pattern in Play
Moonbeams — comfort as a purchase
Pattern Tag
MOONBEAMS (primary) · RAINBOWS (secondary)
Mechanic Introduced
Three-choice structure. Pattern-powered C option.
Hermit Role
End-of-scene reflection question only — no dialogue
Art Used
The Moon's Apartment — late night city
The scene's single question for the player

Is the thing you're buying the thing you actually want — or are you buying the feeling you think the thing will produce?

The Moon doesn't ask this directly. She demonstrates it. That's the job.

Before the Dialogue

Scene Setup

This narration appears on screen before the first dialogue interaction. Short. Sets the vibe. The Moon is already present — the Fool finds her, not the other way around.

The apartment is warm in the way late nights get warm. Low light. Phone screen glow. The particular quiet of a Friday that should feel like relief but hasn't quite landed yet.
The Moon is here. She's always here on Fridays.
She doesn't look up when you come in.
Craft note: The Moon never "arrives." She's already there. This is her natural habitat — the gap between wanting to decompress and actually being able to. The Fool walks into that gap.

What each arrival choice signals

A ("What is it?"): Curiosity. The Fool is open. This is the most common path — leads directly to the cart scene.

B ("I already know..."): Mild resistance, but still here. Leads to the cart scene with a shifted Moon opener — she's amused, not deterred.

C ("You always show up on Fridays"): Pattern recognition active. Skips the cart entirely and leads to the reflective branch. This is the earned path.

Beat Four — All Paths

The Hermit

Every branch ends with The Hermit. He doesn't arrive as a character — just a warm light in the corner and a question on screen. No response box. No dialogue. The question just sits there.

The question changes based on the path the player took through the scene.

○ After Choice A — The Pull
"What were you actually going for?"
No answer required. The question is the experience.
○ After Choice B — The Friction
"What did you want the thing to do for you?"
No answer required. Plants a seed for the Saturday morning reflection.
○ After Choice C — The Pattern (either version)
"What does Friday night feel like when it's actually good?"
No answer required. This one's for after the weekend — the callback arrives in the Sunday closing scene.
Design principle

The Hermit never summarizes the scene. He never says "you did well" or "interesting choice." He only moves the question forward — not backward. His questions are always about what comes next, not what just happened.

Writing Notes

Things to Hold Onto

On The Moon's voice throughout: She never raises it. She never pressures. She genuinely believes you deserve comfort — and she's not wrong. The tension in this scene isn't between good and bad. It's between what you want right now and what you wanted when you started the weekend.
On the cart scene opener variation: The two versions of The Moon's cart opener (after A vs after B) should feel meaningfully different. After A, she's explaining. After B, she's pivoting. Same destination, different approach. That's the Moon — she's flexible.
On Choice C timing: Choice C should never feel like the "correct" answer — even when the player recognizes the pattern, the pull is still real. The Moon going still when named is a reward, not a punishment. The player should feel like they did something precise, not something virtuous.
On the Hermit: He does not appear as a character in this scene. No portrait. No dialogue header. Just the glow and the question. The visual design should reinforce this — he's ambient, not present.
The line this scene must not cross

At no point does the game tell the Fool that Friday night impulse shopping is bad. The Moon is not a villain. Choice A is not a failure. The scene just records what happened and asks one question. The player does all the meaning-making themselves.

"You can't do this wrong."
