## What C3 Is

C3 is a decision framework for spending. Three letters, three options, zero guilt.

**CUT**: Reduce how often you do something. Keep it, but less.
**CANCEL**: Stop paying entirely. Or downgrade to a cheaper version.
**COMBINE**: Share costs, consolidate services, or replace expensive with affordable.

That's the whole framework. Everything else in this guide is application.

---

## Why C3 Exists

Most spending advice falls into two camps: "track everything and feel bad about it" or "manifest abundance and stop worrying." Neither is useful.

C3 exists because people who are paying attention to their spending need a middle step between "I noticed a pattern" and "I changed something." Noticing is important. Changing requires a concrete next move.

C3 gives you three concrete next moves. Pick the one that fits. Apply it. See what happens.

---

## The Three Paths in Detail

### CUT (Reduce Frequency)

CUT means you keep the thing. You just do it less.

This works when something genuinely adds value to your life but the frequency has drifted. You do not want it gone. You want it back in proportion.

**The question to ask:** "Would doing this less often still give me what I need from it?"

**Examples:**

- Coffee shop visits from 5 times a week to 2
- Takeout from every week to twice a month
- Online shopping sessions from whenever to one planned browsing window per week
- New course purchases: finish one before buying another
- Impulse purchases under $25 get a 24-hour wait

**CUT works best when:**
- You get genuine value from the spending
- The problem is frequency, not the thing itself
- Total elimination would feel punishing
- You need friction, not a wall

**CUT does not work when:**
- You do not use the thing at all (that's a CANCEL situation)
- The spending happens automatically without you choosing it
- Reducing frequency won't actually change the cost (fixed subscriptions)

---

### CANCEL (Stop or Downgrade)

CANCEL means you stop paying. Either entirely, or you move to a lower tier.

This works when you are paying for something you forgot about, barely use, or could replace with a free alternative. It also works when a premium version is not delivering enough extra value over the basic version.

**The question to ask:** "If I canceled this today, what would I actually lose?"

**Examples:**

- Unused gym membership: canceled
- Premium Spotify: downgraded to free tier with ads
- Three streaming services: kept one, canceled two
- Magazine subscription: canceled (not reading them)
- Premium project management tool: switched to free version
- Stock photo subscription: canceled (using free alternatives)
- Extended warranties: canceled

**CANCEL works best when:**
- You are paying for something you are not using
- A free or cheaper version would serve you just as well
- You are keeping it out of guilt, habit, or forgetting
- The cost no longer matches the value

**CANCEL does not work when:**
- You actively use and need the service
- There is no free or cheaper alternative that works
- Canceling would create a bigger problem than the cost solves

**The guilt trap:** Most people resist CANCEL because of sunk cost. "I already paid for six months." That money is spent whether you keep using it or not. The question is whether you want to keep spending more.

---

### COMBINE (Consolidate or Share)

COMBINE means you keep the function but change how you pay for it. Share costs, merge duplicate tools, or swap expensive for affordable.

This works when you are paying solo for something that could be shared, when multiple tools do the same job, or when a cheaper option would work just as well.

**The question to ask:** "Is there a way to get this same result for less money?"

**Examples:**

- Solo Netflix plan becomes a family plan split with your household
- Three productivity tools become one that handles all three jobs
- Individual phone plans become a family plan
- Buying new becomes borrowing, secondhand, or using what you have
- Expensive branded items become generic alternatives
- Separate hosting accounts become one consolidated plan
- Multiple email marketing platforms become one

**COMBINE works best when:**
- You are paying for duplicate tools or services
- Someone else could share the cost
- A less expensive alternative exists and works well enough
- Multiple small costs could be replaced by one efficient cost

**COMBINE does not work when:**
- The cheaper alternative is genuinely worse in ways that matter
- Sharing would create friction in important relationships
- Consolidating would mean losing features you actually use daily

## How to Use C3 in Practice

### Step 1: Pick One Category

Do not try to C3 your entire life in one afternoon. Pick one spending category. Subscriptions are the easiest starting point because they show up on your bank statement every month.

### Step 2: List What You Are Paying For

Write down every recurring charge in that category. Include the amount, how often you are billed, and when you last used it. Be honest about the "last used" column. "I think I used it a few weeks ago" is different from "I logged in yesterday."

### Step 3: Assign a C3 Action

For each item, pick one:

- **CUT**: I use this, but I could use it less or downgrade
- **CANCEL**: I do not use this enough to justify the cost
- **COMBINE**: I could share this, merge it with something else, or find a cheaper version
- **KEEP**: This is aligned. The cost matches the value. No change needed.

KEEP is a valid option. Not everything needs to be cut, canceled, or combined. The point is making that choice deliberately instead of by default.

### Step 4: Do the Easiest One First

Look at your list. Find the item where the C3 action is most obvious and least emotionally charged. Do that one first. Today if possible.

This builds momentum. One cancellation, one downgrade, one shared plan. It takes five minutes and changes your relationship with the rest of the list.

### Step 5: Schedule the Rest

Put the remaining actions on a calendar. One per day or one per week. Whatever pace keeps you moving without overwhelming you. The goal is completion over the next 30 days, not perfection by Friday.

## C3 for Common Spending Categories

### Subscriptions and Software

This is where most people find the quickest wins.

**Audit questions:**
- When did I last log in?
- Could the free version do what I need?
- Am I paying for features I do not use?
- Do I have two tools that do the same thing?

**Common C3 moves:**
- CUT: Premium tier to basic tier
- CANCEL: Anything not logged into in 60+ days
- COMBINE: Multiple tools in the same category into one

### Food and Dining

This category is emotional. Proceed with self-awareness.

**Audit questions:**
- How many times this month did I eat out or order in?
- Which of those were planned and which were reactive?
- What was I feeling when I ordered?

**Common C3 moves:**
- CUT: Reduce takeout frequency (not eliminate)
- CANCEL: Meal kit services you are not fully using
- COMBINE: Cook larger batches, share meals with neighbors, meal prep once for the week

### Shopping and Impulse Purchases

The hardest category because the triggers are everywhere.

**Audit questions:**
- What did I buy this month that I did not plan to buy?
- How many of those purchases were driven by an ad, a sale, or boredom?
- If I had waited 48 hours, would I still have bought it?

**Common C3 moves:**
- CUT: Implement a waiting period (24-48 hours for non-essentials)
- CANCEL: Unsubscribe from promotional emails and unfollow brands that trigger spending
- COMBINE: Use what you have, borrow, buy secondhand

### Business Tools and Professional Development

For business owners and freelancers. This category grows quietly.

**Audit questions:**
- Am I paying for tools I bought when my business was at a different stage?
- Do I have overlapping software?
- When was the last time I finished a course before buying a new one?

**Common C3 moves:**
- CUT: One new course or tool per quarter maximum
- CANCEL: Software not used in the last billing cycle
- COMBINE: Consolidate to all-in-one platforms where possible

## C3 and the No-Spend Challenge

If you are doing a 30-day No-Spend Challenge, C3 is the action layer.

The challenge helps you notice patterns. What you reach for, when, and why. That is the awareness work.

C3 is what you do with that awareness. When a spending urge shows up, you have three choices beyond "resist it with willpower" or "give in and feel bad."

You can CUT (do it less often). You can CANCEL (stop doing it). You can COMBINE (find a cheaper path to the same result).

This replaces the all-or-nothing thinking that makes most spending challenges fail. You are not trying to never spend again. You are trying to spend with your eyes open.

**In your daily check-in**, when you log a spend or a spending urge, add one line:

"C3 strategy for next time: CUT / CANCEL / COMBINE"

Circle one. Over 30 days, you will see which strategy your spending patterns respond to most. That is data you can use long after the challenge ends.

## C3 Decision Flowchart

When you are looking at an expense and are not sure which C3 path fits, work through these questions in order.

**1. Do I use this regularly?**
- No, barely or never. CANCEL it.
- Yes, move to question 2.

**2. Could someone else share the cost?**
- Yes. COMBINE (family plan, group rate, cost split).
- No, move to question 3.

**3. Do I have duplicates of this?**
- Yes. COMBINE into one tool or service.
- No, move to question 4.

**4. Is the frequency out of proportion?**
- Yes. CUT to a lower frequency.
- No, move to question 5.

**5. Is there a cheaper version that works just as well?**
- Yes. COMBINE (replace with cheaper alternative) or CANCEL premium, downgrade to basic.
- No. KEEP. This expense is aligned.

## When C3 Does Not Apply

Not every expense fits into CUT, CANCEL, or COMBINE. Some expenses are:

- Essential and already at the right level
- One-time necessities (car repair, medical bill, business license)
- Already optimized
- Worth the cost for what they provide

The framework is not about forcing every dollar into a category. It is about building the habit of asking the question. "Could I CUT, CANCEL, or COMBINE this?" If the answer is "no, this is fine as is," you move on.

Awareness without action is still progress. Not every question needs to result in a change.

## Tracking Your C3 Progress

You do not need a complicated system. A simple list works.

| Date | Item | C3 Action | Monthly Savings | Notes |
|------|------|-----------|-----------------|-------|
| | | CUT / CANCEL / COMBINE / KEEP | $ | |

Fill this in as you make decisions. At the end of 30 days, add up the Monthly Savings column. That number is real money back in your life.

Most people who run through their subscriptions alone find $50-$300 per month in charges they forgot about, do not use, or could downgrade. That is $600-$3,600 per year. From one afternoon of honest evaluation.

## What Happens After 30 Days

C3 is not a one-time exercise. Spending patterns shift. New subscriptions appear. Life changes and so do your needs.

Run a C3 audit quarterly. Pull your bank statement, look at every recurring charge, and ask the three questions again. What was aligned three months ago might not be aligned now.

The framework stays the same. The answers change. That is the point.

## Quick Reference

**CUT** = Reduce frequency. Keep it, do it less.
**CANCEL** = Stop or downgrade. Free yourself from what is not serving you.
**COMBINE** = Share, consolidate, or swap for cheaper. Same result, less cost.

Three options. No shame. Just decisions.
